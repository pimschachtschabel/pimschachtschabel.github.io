Architect of Curiosity

What if curiosity isn't something you find — but something you build?

I'm Pim Schachtschabel — an experience designer, speaker, and author who helps people and organizations design the conditions where wonder becomes possible.

We live in a world that rewards certainty — fast answers, proven methods, predictable outcomes. But the most alive people, teams, and experiences share something that certainty can't produce: wonder.

I've spent over a decade building places where curiosity comes alive — escape rooms, World Expo pavilions, immersive brand experiences. And then I got curious about curiosity itself. What I found, through 75+ research conversations across cultures and disciplines, is that curiosity is not a personality trait. It's a quality of attention. And like anything worth designing, it can be practiced, trained, and built.

About

Pim Schachtschabel

Architect of Curiosity

I design experiences that make people curious — and then I got curious about curiosity itself.

That's probably the simplest way to tell this story. The longer version goes something like this:

I trained as an architect at Delft University of Technology, where I learned that the shape of a room determines what happens inside it. The height of a ceiling changes whether you feel expansive or intimate. The placement of a door determines where your eye goes first. The quality of light decides whether you linger or rush through. I became fascinated not just by how spaces look, but by how they feel — and what you could make people feel if you designed with enough care.

That fascination led me in directions no one predicted. In 2014, I co-founded Sherlocked — what became the #1 escape room in Amsterdam and one of the most acclaimed in Europe. We designed experiences where strangers walked into a room as individuals and walked out as collaborators, having solved mysteries together that they couldn't have solved alone. I learned more about human curiosity in those rooms than in any classroom: how people open up when they feel safe, how a well-placed question can change the energy of a group, how the moment of almost knowing is more powerful than the answer itself.

From there, the work kept expanding. I designed Wild Dining — an experience that reimagined the dinner ritual in unexpected locations, including the toilets of a moving train. I co-created Stone Soup, an international community bringing creators together to prototype experiences. I designed a royal experience for the Dutch King. And I joined Tellart as Design Director, where I now lead holistic experience design for major cultural and brand projects — most recently directing the guest experience for both the Netherlands Pavilion and the Philippine Pavilion at World Expo 2025 in Osaka.

Along the way, the client list grew: Google, Heinz, ABN-AMRO, Rijksmuseum, JDE, Adyen, The Social Hub. Each project taught me something about the mechanics of wonder — what makes people lean in, what makes them forget they're being designed for, what makes an experience stay in someone's body long after they've left the room.

And then the pandemic happened.

In April 2020, locked down in San Francisco, I started a conversation with my collaborator Anthony Rocco that became the most important project of my life. We asked a simple question: Can curiosity be designed — not just in a room, but in a life?

Over the next year, together with Monica Canfield-Lenfest, we held more than 75 carefully designed conversations — we called them Interview Rituals — with people across cultures, backgrounds, and disciplines. These weren't standard interviews. They were experiences: each guest brought a meaningful song, we listened together, and then we explored four curated questions about how curiosity shows up in their lives. What we found changed everything.

The most alive, creative, and present people we spoke with didn't have more answers. They had a different quality of attention. They had learned to observe without grasping, to open without losing themselves, to practice without performing, and to hold their lives with beauty and ease.

From those conversations, six principles emerged — Observing, Suspending, Apprenticing, Ritualizing, Gracing, Flowing — that form the framework of our upcoming book, Architecting Curiosity, and the foundation of everything I now teach and speak about.

These days, my work has a single throughline: helping people and organizations design the conditions where curiosity comes alive. Sometimes that means designing a World Expo pavilion. Sometimes it means facilitating a workshop where a leadership team rediscovers how to ask real questions. Sometimes it means standing on a stage and inviting 500 people to close their eyes and actually notice what they're sensing right now.

The common thread is the belief that curiosity is not a personality trait — it's a quality of attention. And like any quality of attention, it can be practiced, trained, and designed. You don't need to be a naturally curious person. You need to be someone willing to begin.

I live in Amsterdam. I play the cello, climb rocks outdoors, and meditate most mornings. I believe a flower doesn't have to try to be a flower — it just is. And I believe the same is true for your curiosity.

Architecting Curiosity

Designing a Life of Wonder

By Pim Schachtschabel & Anthony Rocco

What if curiosity isn't a personality trait — but a quality of attention that can be designed?

Born from more than 75 carefully designed conversations and a decade of building the world's most immersive experiences, Architecting Curiosity offers six guiding principles for bringing wonder back into daily life. Not as concepts to understand, but as practices to embody — in your morning routine, your work, your relationships, and the quiet moments in between.

This is not a self-help book. It is a space to enter, a practice to inhabit, and an invitation to architect a life where wonder is not the exception — but the architecture.

The 6 Principles

Sowing

Observing

Using all your senses to notice what is around you in the moment

Suspending

Temporarily being open to the possibility of something new

Growing

Apprenticing

Using your beginner's eyes, mind, and heart to learn by doing

Ritualizing

Intentionally exploring a sequence of activities

Letting Go

Gracing

Acting with beauty, love, humor, and ease

Flowing

Moving smoothly and continuously from moment to moment

And then: Rest. Integration. And the spiral begins again.

What Makes This Book Different

Other books explain why curiosity matters. This one teaches you how to design it.

Written by an architect and experience designer who has literally built spaces where curiosity comes alive, Architecting Curiosity approaches wonder the way an architect approaches a building: as something that can be designed. Not controlled — you can't control curiosity any more than you can control the weather. But you can build structures that invite it. You can design the spaces of your life so that wonder has somewhere to land.
